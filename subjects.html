BasicCore concept: Cell Structure

Cell structure and function

All living organisms are built from cells. A cell contains a membrane, cytoplasm, and genetic material. In biology, understanding the cell is crucial because it is the smallest unit that still performs life functions.

The plasma membrane controls movement of substances in and out of the cell, while organelles like mitochondria produce energy. Plant cells also include a cell wall and chloroplasts for photosynthesis.
Thinking prompt:

What role does the cell membrane play when glucose enters a cell?

Think about how the membrane lets some molecules through while blocking others.
Glucose needs an entry path: what kind of transport can move it across the membrane?
The membrane is selective, not just open. It helps the cell use only the right molecules.
MidCore concept: Photosynthesis

How plants convert light into energy

Photosynthesis happens in chloroplasts. Light energy converts carbon dioxide and water into glucose and oxygen. This process powers plant growth and supports food chains.

The light reactions capture energy and split water molecules, releasing oxygen. The Calvin cycle uses that energy to build glucose from CO2. Both stages are essential for plant energy storage.
Thinking prompt:

Why do plants release oxygen during photosynthesis even though they need energy?

Oxygen is a by-product of one of the chemical steps. Which molecule splits to produce it?
Water is split during the light reactions, releasing electrons and oxygen.
The plant keeps the energy it needs in glucose and releases oxygen because it cannot store it in the same way.
CrucialCore concept: Genetics

DNA, genes, and inheritance

Genetics explains how traits pass from parents to offspring. DNA contains genes, sections that code proteins. Mutations can change traits, and understanding inheritance reveals why families share features.

Chromosomes carry many genes. During reproduction, each parent contributes half the DNA. Dominant and recessive traits determine which gene variants are expressed in the offspring.
Thinking prompt:

How does a change in a single gene affect an organism differently than a change in many genes?

Think about whether one gene or many genes control a single characteristic.
A single gene may change one protein, while many genes can influence a whole pathway.
Single-gene changes often affect one trait; many-gene changes can affect complex features like height or metabolism.
