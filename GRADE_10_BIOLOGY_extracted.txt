1.0 CELL BIOLOGY AND CELL BIODIVERSITY 
Cell biology is the branch of biology that deals with the study of cells, which are the 
basic structural and functional units of life. It focuses on the structure, functions, 
and processes of cells such as growth and division. Cell biodiversity refers to the 
wide variety of cells found in living organisms, which di Ưer in shape, size, structure, 
and function. This diversity enables specialization of cells, allowing organisms to 
perform di Ưerent life processes e Ưiciently.

Procedures for Collecting, Processing, and Preserving Biological 
Specimens Found in a School Laboratory  
Using only apparatus typically found in a school biology lab (e.g., test tubes, 
pipettes, petri dishes, microscopes, slides, forceps, beakers, alcohol, gloves, 
etc.), here are detailed procedures for common biological specimens: 
 
1. Plant Leaf Specimens 
Apparatus Used:  
 Forceps 
 Scissors 
 Slides and cover slips 
 Microscope 
 Petri dishes 
 Alcohol (70% for temporary preservation) 
Procedure:  
1. Select a healthy leaf. 
2. Use scissors and forceps to cut a small section. 
3. Place the section in a drop of water on a slide. 
4. Add a cover slip and examine under a microscope. 
5. For temporary preservation, place leaf in a Petri dish with 70% alcohol. 
6. Label the Petri dish with specimen name and date. 
 
2. Onion Epidermal Cells (for Microscopy) 
Apparatus Used:  
 Razor blade 
 Forceps 
 Slides and cover slips 
 Iodine solution (staining) 
 Microscope 
Procedure:  
1. Peel a thin layer of onion epidermis using forceps. 
2. Place the epidermis on a slide. 
3. Add a drop of iodine solution to stain nuclei. 
4. Cover with a cover slip carefully to avoid air bubbles. 
5. Observe under a microscope. 
6. Dispose of after observation or store slides temporarily in a slide box. 
 
3. Pond Water Specimens (Microorganisms) 
Apparatus Used:

Making a Herbarium to Preserve Plant Specimens  
A herbarium is a collection of preserved plant specimens used for study and 
reference. In a school laboratory, the procedure involves collecting, 
pressing, drying, mounting, labeling, and storing specimens properly . 
 
Apparatus Used 
 Plant specimens (leaves, ﬂowers, stems) 
 Newspaper or blotting paper 
 Cardboard or corrugated sheets 
 Plant press (or improvised press with heavy books) 
 Forceps and scissors 
 Glue or adhesive tape 
 Herbarium sheets (thick paper or cardboard) 
 Labels 
 Pencil or pen 
 Cabinets or cupboards for storage 
 
Procedure 

--- PAGE 12 ---
 
Page | 12 1. Collecting Specimens  
 Choose healthy, representative plant parts (leaves, ﬂowers, seeds, fruits). 
 Avoid damaged or diseased parts. 
 Collect specimens carefully using scissors or forceps. 
2. Pressing  
 Place the specimen between layers of newspaper or blotting paper. 
 Arrange leaves and ﬂowers ﬂat and spread out naturally. 
 Stack cardboard sheets or place in a plant press. 
 Tighten the press to apply even pressure. 
3. Drying  
 Keep the pressed specimens in a dry, ventilated area. 
 Change the newspaper regularly to avoid mold growth. 
 Drying may take 1–2 weeks depending on specimen thickness and moisture. 
4. Mounting  
 Once dried, carefully glue or tape the specimen onto a herbarium sheet. 
 Ensure all parts are secured without overlapping important structures. 
5. Labeling  
 Include the following information on a label attached to the sheet: 
o Local/Common name and scientiﬁc name 
o Date of collection 
o Locality (place where collected) 
o Collector’s name (optional) 
 Write clearly with pencil or archival-quality pen to prevent fading. 
6. Storage and Protection  
 Store mounted herbarium sheets in a dry cabinet or cupboard. 
 Keep away from direct sunlight, moisture, and pests. 
 For long-term protection, sheets can be placed in plastic covers or archival 
folders. 
 
Tips for a Good Herbarium 
 Always collect multiple specimens for the same plant to show variation. 
 Include ﬂowers and fruits when possible for identiﬁcation. 
 Handle dried specimens gently to avoid breakage. 
 Regularly check stored specimens for pests or mold. 
Collecting Animals in a School Laboratory Using Appropriate Apparatus  
In a school biology lab or ﬁeld studies, small animals and insects can be 
collected using di Ưerent tools depending on their habitat and behavior. 
Below is a detailed guide on collection using common school lab apparatus. 
 
1. Pooter (Suction Device for Small Insects) 
Apparatus Used:  
 Pooter (mouth-operated suction jar with two tubes) 
 Collection vials or Petri dishes 
Procedure:  
1. Place the tip of the pooter near the insect. 
2. Suck gently through the mouthpiece to draw the insect into the container. 
3. Release into a vial or Petri dish for observation. 

--- PAGE 13 ---
 
Page | 13 4. Avoid injuring delicate specimens. 
 
2. Pitfall Trap (For Ground-Dwelling Insects and Small Animals) 
Apparatus Used:  
 Small container (cup, jar) 
 Soil or leaf litter 
 Covering (optional, to prevent rainwater) 
 Preservative (optional, e.g., alcohol for long-term storage) 
Procedure:  
1. Dig a hole and place the container so its rim is level with the ground. 
2. Add a small amount of preservative if the specimens will not be checked daily. 
3. Cover lightly with leaves or a small lid to prevent debris from falling in. 
4. Leave for 24–48 hours. 
5. Collect trapped animals carefully using forceps or a brush. 
 
3. Forceps (Direct Collection of Small Animals) 
Apparatus Used:  
 Forceps 
 Petri dishes or vials 
 Gloves 
Procedure:  
1. Identify small animals (insects, worms, snails). 
2. Pick them gently with forceps. 
3. Place them in a Petri dish or container for observation. 
4. Avoid squeezing to prevent injury. 
 
4. Sweep Net (For Flying or Vegetation-Dwelling Insects) 
Apparatus Used:  
 Sweep net (long-handled net with ﬁne mesh) 
 Collection jar or vial 
 Gloves 
Procedure:  
1. Sweep the net through long grass, shrubs, or low vegetation. 
2. After each sweep, invert the net and shake insects into a jar or vial. 
3. Observe or temporarily store in a Petri dish. 
 
5. Light Traps (For Nocturnal Insects like Moths) 
Apparatus Used:  
 Light source (UV lamp or strong white light) 
 White sheet 
 Collection jar 
Procedure:  
1. Set up a white sheet near the light source at night. 
2. Insects attracted to the light will land on the sheet. 
3. Collect insects using forceps or pooter. 
4. Transfer to jars for observation or study. 
 

--- PAGE 14 ---
 
Page | 14 6. Tullgren Funnel (For Soil or Leaf Litter Arthropods) 
Apparatus Used:  
 Tullgren funnel 
 Light source 
 Collection jar 
 Leaf litter or soil sample 
Procedure:  
1. Place leaf litter or soil in the funnel. 
2. Position a light above the sample. 
3. As the sample dries from the heat/light, arthropods move downward and fall into 
the collection jar. 
4. Collect and observe specimens in the jar. 
 
7. Bait Trap (For Ants, Flies, and Other Scavengers) 
Apparatus Used:  
 Small container 
 Bait (sugar, fruit, meat) 
 Forceps or brush 
Procedure:  
1. Place bait in the container and leave in the area where insects are expected. 
2. Check after some hours. 
3. Collect attracted insects using forceps or pooter. 
4. Observe or temporarily store in Petri dishes.

 Allows observation of physiological features (e.g., plant cells, insect anatomy). 
 Helps students and researchers learn identiﬁcation techniques. 
 
2. Documentation and Record Keeping 
 Preserved specimens serve as a permanent record of biodiversity.

 Ensures specimens are safe for handling in educational and research settings. 
 
7. Reference for Future Studies 
 Preserved specimens can be re-examined for future research, even years later. 
 Allows comparison with newly collected specimens to study evolution, 
adaptation, or disease.

Preparation of Slides for Observation Under a Microscope  
Preparing a microscope slide properly is essential to clearly observe the 
specimen’s structure. There are two main types of slides: wet mounts  and 
dry mounts . 
 
Apparatus Used 
 Microscope 
 Slides and cover slips 
 Forceps 
 Dropper or pipette 
 Razor blade or scissors (for cutting specimens) 
 Stains (iodine, methylene blue, or safranin) 
 Water or glycerin (for wet mounts) 
 Paper towels 

--- PAGE 16 ---
 
Page | 16  
1. Wet Mount Slide 
Used for observing living cells, microorganisms, or tissues in liquid . 
Procedure:  
1. Place a clean slide on a ﬂat surface. 
2. Using a dropper, place a small drop of water on the slide. 
3. Place the specimen in the drop of water using forceps. 
4. Carefully lower a cover slip at an angle to avoid air bubbles. 
5. Observe under the microscope starting with low magniﬁcation. 
6. Optionally, add a drop of stain (e.g., iodine) to improve visibility. 
Applications:  
 Pond water microorganisms 
 Live plant cells (e.g., Elodea) 
 Blood cells 
 
2. Dry Mount Slide 
Used for solid specimens or prepared specimens that do not need liquid . 
Procedure:  
1. Place the specimen directly on the center of a clean slide. 
2. If necessary, cut the specimen thinly with a razor blade. 
3. Cover with a cover slip if needed to ﬂatten the specimen. 
4. Observe under the microscope starting with low magniﬁcation. 
Applications:  
 Dried leaves 
 Small insects 
 Hair or ﬁbers 
 
3. Staining of Slides 
Staining is done to enhance contrast and make structures visible . 
Procedure:

Estimation of Cell Size Using Microscopy 
A photomicrograph  is a photograph taken through a microscope  to show a 
magniﬁed image of very small objects , such as cells, tissues, or microorganisms, that 
cannot be seen clearly with the naked eye. 
Key points: 
 It is produced using a microscope ﬁtted with a camera  
 It shows real structures  as they appear under the microscope 
 It is used in biology and medical studies  to observe cell details 
Example: 
A photomicrograph may show plant cells, animal cells, or blood cells under high 
magniﬁcation. 
Note: 
A photomicrograph is di Ưerent from a drawing or diagram, which may simplify or 
exaggerate features. 
 
Estimating the size of cells under a microscope involves careful observation and 
calculation using the microscope’s ocular and objective lenses . Here is a detailed 
guide: 
 
1. Apparatus Required  
 Light microscope with ocular (eyepiece) micrometer  
 Stage micrometer  (a slide with a precisely marked scale, usually 1 mm divided 
into 100 divisions) 
 Prepared cell slides  (e.g., onion epidermis, cheek cells) 
 Lens paper , forceps, and distilled water  if mounting fresh specimens 
 

--- PAGE 19 ---
 
Page | 19 2. Calibration of the Microscope  
To measure cells accurately, ﬁrst calibrate the eyepiece micrometer using the stage 
micrometer. 
Steps: 
1. Place the stage micrometer  on the stage. 
2. Focus the microscope using the objective lens you plan to use for observation. 
3. Align the ocular micrometer scale  with the stage micrometer scale . 
4. Count how many divisions of the ocular micrometer  correspond to a known 
length on the stage micrometer . 
o Example: 10 ocular divisions = 0.1 mm (100 µm) on stage micrometer 
5. Calculate the ocular unit : 
Length of 1 ocular division =known length on stage micrometer
number of ocular divisions 
 
Example: 0.1 mm÷10 = 0.01  mm= 10ௗ𝜇mper division 
 
3. Observing the Cells  
1. Place the cell slide  on the stage. 
2. Focus using the lowest power objective , then switch to higher magniﬁcation  
for detailed measurement. 
3. Align the cell with the ocular micrometer  and count how many divisions span 
the cell’s length or diameter . 
 
4. Calculating Cell Size  
Cell size =number of ocular divisions ×length of 1 ocular division (µm) 
 
Example:  
 Ocular micrometer: 1 division = 10 µm 
 Cell spans 7 divisions 
Cell size = 7×10 = 70 ௗ𝜇m 
 
 
5. Notes for Accuracy  
 Always recalibrate  when changing objectives. 
 Measure multiple cells  and calculate the average size . 
 Avoid parallax error  by viewing directly through the eyepiece. 
 Use fresh, well-prepared slides  to prevent distortion. 
 
6. Optional Variations  
 Using digital microscopes : software can directly measure cell dimensions. 
 Special stains  (like methylene blue) can make cell boundaries clearer for more 
accurate measurement.

 Thin cell wall allows faster movement of water by osmosis

 Many mitochondria provide energy for active transport of mineral ions

Function:  Photosynthesis. 
Adaptations:  
 Packed with many chloroplasts to absorb maximum light

 Located near the upper surface of the leaf where light intensity is highest 
 Thin cell walls allow carbon dioxide to di Ưuse easily

Function:  Control opening and closing of stomata for gas exchange and

Adaptations:  
 Kidney-shaped (in dicot plants) to allow opening and closing of the stoma 
 Unevenly thickened cell walls cause cells to curve when turgid 
 Contain chloroplasts to provide energy for active transport 
 Change turgor pressure to regulate transpiration

Adaptations:

 Light and small for easy dispersal by wind or insects 
 Contains a pollen tube nucleus to grow a pollen tube 
 Contains a generative nucleus to produce male gametes

(b) Test for Reducing Sugars – Benedict’s Test 
Aim: 
To test for the presence of reducing sugars.

 Benedict’s solution 
 Test tubes 
 Water bath 
 Food samples:

o Sugarcane juice

4. Observe the colour change. 
Results: 
 Blue → no reducing sugar 
 Green/yellow/orange/brick-red precipitate → reducing sugar present 
Conclusion:  
A colour change indicates the presence of reducing sugars such as glucose.

Enzymes 
Factors A Ưecting Enzyme Activity  
Enzymes  are biological catalysts  made of proteins that speed up chemical reactions 
in living cells without being used up. 
 
1. Temperature 
EƯect: 


--- PAGE 35 ---
 
Page | 35  As temperature increases, enzyme activity increases up to an optimum 
temperature  
 Beyond the optimum, enzyme activity decreases rapidly  
Explanation: 
 Increase in temperature gives molecules more kinetic energy 
 This increases the frequency of enzyme–substrate collisions  
 At very high temperatures, enzymes become denatured  
 Denaturation changes the shape of the active site, so the substrate can no longer 
ﬁt 
Key Points: 
 Optimum temperature in humans is about 37°C 
 Low temperatures slow reactions but do not denature enzymes  
 High temperatures permanently damage enzymes 
 
2. pH 
EƯect: 
 Each enzyme has a speciﬁc optimum pH  
 Enzyme activity decreases if pH is too acidic or too alkaline 
Explanation: 
 pH aƯects the charge and shape of the active site  
 Extreme pH values cause denaturation  
 This prevents formation of the enzyme–substrate complex 
Examples: 
 Pepsin works best in acidic conditions (pH ~2) 
 Amylase  works best at neutral pH (pH ~7) 
 Trypsin works best in alkaline conditions (pH ~8) 
 
3. Substrate Concentration 
EƯect: 
 Increasing substrate concentration increases enzyme activity up to a maximum 
 After a certain point, the rate remains constant 
Explanation: 
 More substrate molecules increase chances of collision with enzyme active sites 
 At high substrate concentration, all enzyme active sites become occupied  
 The enzyme becomes the limiting factor  
Key Term: 
 Saturation point : when all active sites are ﬁlled 
 
4. Enzyme Concentration 
EƯect: 
 Increasing enzyme concentration increases the rate of reaction (if substrate is 
suƯicient) 
Explanation: 
 More enzymes mean more active sites available 
 Reaction rate increases proportionally until substrate becomes limiting 
 
5. Presence of Inhibitors 

--- PAGE 36 ---
 
Page | 36 EƯect: 
 Inhibitors reduce or stop enzyme activity 
Types of Inhibitors: 
(a) Competitive Inhibitors 
 Compete with substrate for the active site 
 Similar shape to the substrate 
 Can be overcome by increasing substrate concentration 
(b) Non-competitive Inhibitors 
 Bind to a di Ưerent part of the enzyme  
 Change the shape of the active site 
 Cannot be overcome by increasing substrate concentration 
 
6. Presence of Cofactors and Coenzymes 
EƯect: 
 Some enzymes require cofactors to function properly 
Explanation: 
 Cofactors may be: 
o Metal ions  (e.g. Mg² ⁺, Fe²⁺) 
o Coenzymes  (organic molecules, often vitamins) 
 Without cofactors, the enzyme remains inactive 
 
7. Product Concentration 
EƯect: 
 Accumulation of products can slow down enzyme activity 
Explanation: 
 Products may inhibit the enzyme 
 This is known as feedback inhibition  
 Helps regulate metabolic pathways

PROCESS OF PHOTOSYNTHESIS  
 Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants manufacture their own food 
using light energy. 
 It takes place mainly in the leaves, inside cell organelles called chloroplasts. 
 Chlorophyll in the chloroplasts traps light energy from the sun. 
Stage 1: Light-Dependent Reactions  
 Occur in the grana of the chloroplast. 
 Sunlight is absorbed by chlorophyll. 
 Light energy splits water molecules into hydrogen and oxygen. 
 Oxygen is released into the atmosphere as a by-product. 
 Energy is stored in the form of ATP and NADPH. 
Stage 2: Light-Independent Reactions Calvin Cycle  
 Occur in the stroma of the chloroplast. 
 Carbon dioxide from the air enters the leaf through stomata. 
 Hydrogen and energy from ATP and NADPH are used to reduce carbon dioxide. 
 Glucose is formed as the main food substance. 
 Excess glucose is converted into starch for storage. 
Raw Materials Required  
 Carbon dioxide from the air. 
 Water from the soil. 
 Light energy from the sun. 
 Chlorophyll. 
Products of Photosynthesis  
 Glucose used for energy and growth. 
 Oxygen released into the atmosphere. 
Word Equation  
 Carbon dioxide + Water → Glucose + Oxygen 
Balanced Chemical Equation

ATP 
 Full meaning:  Adenosine Triphosphate 
 Meaning:  
o ATP is the main energy carrier in living cells. 
o It stores and supplies energy needed for cellular activities such as

o In photosynthesis, ATP provides energy for the light-independent

Summary  
 ATP provides energy. 
 NADPH provides hydrogen and electrons.

Structure of Roots Related to Transport  
 Root cap protects the growing tip. 
 Root hairs are long, thin outgrowths of epidermal cells. 
 Cortex is made of loosely packed cells.

Functions of Roots in Transport  
 Absorption of water from the soil.

 Conduction of absorbed water and minerals to the stem.

 Thin walls of root hairs allow easy di Ưusion and osmosis.

 Endodermis regulates selective uptake of minerals.

Structure of Stems Related to Transport  
 Vascular bundles arranged in a ring in dicot stems. 
 Xylem is located on the inner side of the vascular bundle. 
 Phloem is located on the outer side of the vascular bundle. 
 Presence of nodes and internodes for leaf attachment. 
Functions of Stems in Transport  
 Transport of water and mineral salts from roots to leaves. 
 Transport of manufactured food from leaves to other parts of the plant. 
 Support of leaves, ﬂowers and fruits. 
Adaptations of Stems for Transport  
 Long continuous xylem vessels for uninterrupted water ﬂow. 
 Presence of lignin in xylem walls for strength and prevention of collapse. 
 Phloem sieve tubes allow bidirectional movement of food. 
 Vascular bundles extend throughout the stem for e Ưicient distribution.

 Broad and ﬂat lamina. 
 Veins containing xylem and phloem. 
 Palisade mesophyll cells packed with chloroplasts. 
 Spongy mesophyll with air spaces.

ARRANGEMENT OF VASCULAR TISSUES IN MONOCOTYLEDONOUS AND 
DICOTYLEDONOUS PLANTS  
MONOCOTYLEDONOUS PLANTS  
 Vascular bundles are scattered throughout the ground tissue of the stem. 
 Each vascular bundle is closed, meaning there is no cambium between xylem 
and phloem. 
 Xylem is positioned on the inner side of each vascular bundle. 
 Phloem is positioned on the outer side of each vascular bundle. 
 This arrangement limits secondary growth. 
 Examples include maize, wheat and grasses. 
DICOTYLEDONOUS PLANTS  
 Vascular bundles are arranged in a ring near the outer edge of the stem. 
 Each vascular bundle is open, with a cambium layer present between xylem and 
phloem. 
 Xylem faces the center of the stem. 
 Phloem faces the outer side of the stem. 
 Presence of cambium allows secondary growth and stem thickening. 
 Examples include beans, sunﬂower and mango.

Uptake of Water and Mineral Salts in Plants  
1. Absorption of Water and Mineral Salts  
 Water and dissolved minerals are absorbed mainly through root hairs , which 
increase the surface area for absorption. 
 Mineral salts are absorbed by active transport , which requires energy, while 
water moves by osmosis . 
2. Mechanisms of Water Movement  
a) Root Pressure  
 Deﬁnition:  Upward pressure in the xylem generated by active absorption of 
minerals and water in the roots. 
 Process:  
o Mineral salts are actively absorbed into the xylem. 
o Water follows by osmosis, creating a positive pressure that pushes water 
up the xylem. 
 Signiﬁcance:  Helps move water a short distance up the plant, especially at night 
or when transpiration is low. 
 Observation:  Can be observed as guttation  (droplets at leaf edges). 
b) Capillarity  
 Deﬁnition:  The rise of water in narrow tubes due to adhesion  (water sticks to 
walls) and cohesion  (water sticks to itself). 
 Signiﬁcance:  Contributes slightly to water movement in xylem vessels. 

--- PAGE 53 ---
 
Page | 53  More eƯective in small diameter vessels . 
c) Transpiration Pull  
 Deﬁnition:  Upward pull of water from roots to leaves caused by transpiration  
(loss of water vapor) from leaf stomata. 
 Process:  
o Water evaporates from mesophyll cells into leaf air spaces and exits 
through stomata. 
o This creates tension in the xylem, pulling water upward. 
o Water molecules stick together (cohesion) and to xylem walls (adhesion), 
forming a continuous column. 
 Signiﬁcance:  Main driving force for water transport in tall plants. 
3. Overall Pathway of Water Movement  
1. Water enters root hairs by osmosis . 
2. Water moves across root cortex to xylem. 
3. Water is pulled up the stem by root pressure, capillarity, and mainly 
transpiration pull . 
4. Water reaches leaves, where it participates in photosynthesis  or evaporates via 
transpiration.

 Light intensity:

 Wind/air movement:

b) Plant Factors

 Water and mineral transport:  Creates transpiration pull, helping water and

 Cooling:  Evaporation of water from leaves lowers leaf temperature, preventing

Translocation in Plants  
Deﬁnition  
Translocation is the movement of manufactured food (mainly sugars such as 
sucrose) from the leaves, where it is produced by photosynthesis, to other parts 
of the plant where it is needed for growth, storage, or respiration. This movement 
takes place through the phloem tissue . 
Substances Translocated  
 Sucrose (main transported sugar) 
 Amino acids 
 Plant hormones 
 Some ions and organic solutes 
Direction of Translocation  
 Translocation is bidirectional : food can move both upwards and downwards 
depending on the plant’s needs. 
 Movement is from sources to sinks: 
o Source: parts that produce or release food (e.g. mature leaves). 
o Sink: parts that use or store food (e.g. roots, growing buds, fruits, seeds, 
storage organs). 
 
Mechanics of Translocation (Mass Flow / Pressure Flow Hypothesis)  
The most accepted explanation for translocation is the pressure ﬂow 
hypothesis , proposed by Ernst Münch. 
1. Loading of Food at the Source  
 In the leaves, sucrose produced during photosynthesis is actively transported 
into the sieve tube elements  of the phloem. 
 This increases the concentration of sugars in the phloem sap. 
 Water enters the sieve tubes from the xylem by osmosis , raising the turgor 
(hydrostatic) pressure  at the source. 
2. Pressure Gradient Formation  
 High pressure develops at the source region (leaves). 

--- PAGE 55 ---
 
Page | 55  At the sink (roots, fruits, growing tissues), sucrose is removed from the phloem 
for use or storage. 
 Removal of sugar lowers the solute concentration, causing water to leave the 
phloem, reducing pressure at the sink. 
 This creates a pressure gradient  from source (high pressure) to sink (low 
pressure). 
3. Mass Flow of Phloem Sap  
 The pressure di Ưerence causes phloem sap to ﬂow in bulk from the source to the 
sink through the sieve tubes. 
 This ﬂow is not dependent on di Ưusion but on pressure di Ưerences. 
4. Unloading at the Sink  
 Sucrose is actively or passively transported out of the sieve tubes into sink 
tissues. 
 Water then moves back into the xylem or surrounding cells. 
 This maintains the low pressure at the sink, allowing continuous ﬂow. 
 
Key Features of Phloem in Translocation  
 Sieve tube elements : living cells that form continuous tubes for food transport. 
 Companion cells : provide energy (ATP) and metabolic support for active 
transport processes. 
 Sieve plates : perforated end walls that allow sap to pass between sieve tube 
elements. 
 
Summary  
Translocation is the movement of manufactured food in plants through the 
phloem from sources to sinks. It occurs by a mass ﬂow mechanism driven by 
pressure di Ưerences created through active loading and unloading of sugars, 
with water movement by osmosis playing a crucial role. 
 
2.3 Gaseous Exchange and Respiration 
Gaseous Exchange  
Gaseous exchange is the process by which organisms take in oxygen and remove 
carbon dioxide with their environment. It allows oxygen to enter cells for 
respiration and enables carbon dioxide, a waste product of respiration, to be 
released. 
In Plants  
 Gaseous exchange occurs mainly through stomata  in leaves, lenticels  in stems, 
and root hairs  in roots. 
 Oxygen enters plant cells for respiration. 
 Carbon dioxide enters for photosynthesis and is released during respiration. 
 The process occurs by diƯusion, moving gases from an area of higher 
concentration to an area of lower concentration. 
In Animals  
 Gaseous exchange takes place in specialized organs such as: 
o Lungs (alveoli) in mammals 
o Gills in ﬁsh 
o Tracheal system  in insects

Importance of Gaseous Exchange  
 Supplies oxygen needed for aerobic respiration and energy production. 
 Removes carbon dioxide, preventing harmful accumulation. 
 Maintains the balance of gases in the organism. 
Summary  
Gaseous exchange is the di Ưusion-based movement of oxygen into an organism 
and carbon dioxide out of it, essential for respiration and survival. 
Signiﬁcance of Gaseous Exchange  
In Plants  
 Provides oxygen for respiration , which releases energy needed for growth, 
active transport, and cell division. 
 Supplies carbon dioxide for photosynthesis , enabling the manufacture of food. 
 Allows removal of carbon dioxide produced during respiration , preventing toxic 
buildup. 
 Maintains a balance between photosynthesis and respiration, ensuring normal 
plant metabolism. 
 Supports growth of roots, stems, leaves, ﬂowers, and fruits through continuous 
energy supply. 
In Animals  
 Supplies oxygen for aerobic respiration , allowing the release of energy for 
movement, growth, repair, and maintaining body functions. 
 Enables removal of carbon dioxide , a waste product that can be harmful if it 
accumulates in the blood. 
 Helps maintain normal blood pH , since excess carbon dioxide forms carbonic 
acid. 
 Supports vital activities such as heartbeat, muscle contraction, nerve impulse 
transmission, and temperature regulation. 
 Ensures survival by sustaining metabolism and overall body function. 
Summary  
Gaseous exchange is essential in both plants and animals because it ensures a 
continuous supply of oxygen for respiration and the removal of carbon dioxide, 
supporting life processes and maintaining internal balance. 
Aquatic and Terrestrial Plants (Explanation) 
Aquatic plants  are plants that live partly or wholly in water. Examples include

environment. 
Terrestrial plants  are plants that live on land. Examples include maize, beans, 
and trees. Since air is available around them, gaseous exchange occurs mainly 
through specialized structures on leaves, stems, and roots.

Mechanisms of Opening and Closing of Stomata 
Stomata are microscopic pores on the leaf epidermis that regulate gaseous 
exchange and transpiration. Their opening and closing depend on changes in 

--- PAGE 61 ---
 
Page | 61 turgor pressure of the guard cells , which is controlled by metabolic and ionic 
processes. 
 
1. Photosynthetic Theory 
Basic Idea 
This theory states that photosynthesis in guard cells  controls stomatal 
movement by altering carbon dioxide concentration and pH. 
Mechanism of Opening (In Light) 
1. Sunlight stimulates photosynthesis in guard cell chloroplasts . 
2. Carbon dioxide is rapidly used up during photosynthesis. 
3. Reduced CO₂ concentration causes the guard cell sap to become alkaline 
(higher pH) . 
4. Increased pH activates enzymes that convert starch into soluble sugars . 
5. Accumulation of sugars increases the osmotic concentration  of guard cells. 
6. Water enters guard cells from surrounding epidermal cells by osmosis . 
7. Guard cells become turgid. 
8. Due to thick inner walls and thin outer walls, guard cells curve outward. 
9. The stomatal pore opens . 
Mechanism of Closing (In Darkness) 
1. Photosynthesis stops due to lack of light. 
2. Carbon dioxide accumulates from respiration. 
3. Increased CO₂ lowers the pH, making the cell sap acidic. 
4. Sugars are converted back into starch, which is insoluble. 
5. Osmotic concentration of guard cells decreases. 
6. Water moves out of guard cells by osmosis. 
7. Guard cells become ﬂaccid. 
8. The stoma closes . 
Limitations 
 Does not explain stomatal opening at night in some plants. 
 Cannot fully explain stomatal movement when photosynthesis is minimal. 
 
2. Starch–Sugar Interconversion Theory 
Basic Idea 
This theory emphasizes that interconversion between starch and sugar  in 
guard cells controls water movement and hence stomatal movement. 
Mechanism of Opening (In Light) 
1. Light causes a decrease in CO₂ concentration within guard cells. 
2. Decreased CO₂ raises the pH of guard cell sap. 
3. The enzyme phosphorylase  becomes active at high pH. 
4. Stored starch is hydrolysed into glucose and other soluble sugars . 
5. Increase in soluble sugars lowers the water potential  of guard cells. 
6. Water enters by osmosis from surrounding cells. 
7. Guard cells become turgid. 
8. DiƯerential wall thickness causes bending.  
9. The stoma opens. 
Mechanism of Closing (In Darkness) 
1. CO₂ accumulates due to respiration. 

--- PAGE 62 ---
 
Page | 62 2. Guard cell pH decreases. 
3. Enzymes convert sugars back into starch. 
4. Insoluble starch reduces osmotic concentration. 
5. Water potential increases. 
6. Water moves out of guard cells. 
7. Guard cells lose turgidity. 
8. The stoma closes. 
Strengths 
 Clearly explains osmotic changes. 
 Supported by observed starch-sugar changes. 
Limitations 
 Too slow to explain rapid stomatal movement. 
 Does not account for ion involvement. 
 
3. Potassium Ions (K ⁺) Theory (Modern and Most Accepted) 
Basic Idea 
Stomatal movement is controlled by active transport of potassium ions  into 
and out of guard cells, driven by ATP and membrane pumps. 
 
Detailed Mechanism of Opening (In Light) 
1. Light activates ATP-driven proton (H ⁺) pumps in guard cell membranes. 
2. H⁺ ions are actively pumped out into surrounding cells. 
3. Loss of H ⁺ increases the negative electrical potential  inside guard cells. 
4. Potassium ions (K ⁺) enter guard cells through speciﬁc channels. 
5. To maintain electrical balance: 
o Chloride ions (Cl ⁻) enter, or 
o Organic acids (malate² ⁻) are formed from starch. 
6. Accumulation of K ⁺, Cl⁻, and malate greatly lowers the water potential . 
7. Water enters guard cells by osmosis. 
8. Guard cells become highly turgid. 
9. Unequal wall thickness causes guard cells to bow outward. 
10. The stomatal pore opens widely . 
 
Detailed Mechanism of Closing (In Darkness or Water Stress) 
1. Light is absent or plant experiences water stress. 
2. Proton pumps become inactive. 
3. K⁺ ions move out of guard cells into surrounding cells. 
4. Malate is converted back into starch or leaves the cell. 
5. Cl⁻ ions diƯuse out. 
6. Water potential inside guard cells increases. 
7. Water leaves guard cells by osmosis. 
8. Guard cells become ﬂaccid. 
9. The stoma closes . 
 
Additional Factors Supporting K ⁺ Theory 
 Explains rapid stomatal responses. 
 Works even when photosynthesis is low.

 Accounts for stomatal closure during drought (via abscisic acid action).

Respiration  
Respiration is the biochemical process by which living cells break down food 
substances, especially glucose, to release energy needed for life processes. 
Key Points  
 It occurs in all living cells , both plant and animal. 
 The energy released is used for growth, movement, repair, active transport, and 
maintenance of body functions. 
 Respiration is a continuous process  and is not the same as breathing. 
Types of Respiration 
1. Aerobic Respiration 
Deﬁnition:  
Aerobic respiration is the breakdown of glucose in the presence of oxygen  to 
release energy, carbon dioxide, and water. 
Word Equation:  
Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water + Energy 
Chemical Equation:  
C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + Energy (≈ 36–38 ATP) 
Where It Occurs:  
 Cytoplasm (glycolysis) 
 Mitochondria (Krebs cycle and electron transport chain) 
Energy Yield:  
 Produces a large amount of energy  (~36–38 ATP molecules per glucose) 
Examples:  
 Most animals, plants, and aerobic microorganisms. 
Advantages:  
 Maximum energy production. 
 Produces harmless end products (CO₂ and H₂O). 
 
2. Anaerobic Respiration 

--- PAGE 64 ---
 
Page | 64 Deﬁnition:  
Anaerobic respiration is the breakdown of glucose without oxygen , resulting in 
incomplete oxidation and formation of less energy. 
Word Equations:  
 In animals: Glucose → Lactic acid + Energy 
 In plants and yeast: Glucose → Ethanol + Carbon dioxide + Energy 
Chemical Equations:  
 Animals: C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2C₃H₆O₃ + Energy (~2 ATP) 
 Plants/Yeast: C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2C₂H₅OH + 2CO₂ + Energy (~2 ATP) 
Where It Occurs:  
 Cytoplasm only 
Energy Yield:  
 Produces small amount of energy  (≈ 2 ATP per glucose) 
Examples:  
 Muscle cells during intense exercise (lactic acid) 
 Yeast fermentation (ethanol + CO₂) 
 Root cells in waterlogged soil 
Disadvantages:  
 Produces toxic by-products (lactic acid or ethanol) 
 Releases much less energy than aerobic respiration

Signiﬁcance of Gaseous Exchange 
Gaseous exchange is the process by which oxygen is absorbed and carbon 
dioxide is released. Its importance is as follows: 
In Plants 
1. Supplies carbon dioxide (CO₂)  for photosynthesis , enabling production of 
glucose and other organic compounds. 
2. Provides oxygen (O₂)  for respiration , releasing energy for growth, reproduction, 
and other metabolic activities. 
3. Removes excess carbon dioxide  from plant tissues, preventing acidiﬁcation that 
could damage cells. 
4. Maintains a balance between photosynthesis and respiration , essential for 
plant survival. 
In Animals 
1. Provides oxygen for aerobic respiration , which releases energy for movement, 
growth, repair, and maintenance of body temperature. 
2. Removes carbon dioxide , a waste product of respiration, preventing toxicity and 
maintaining blood pH. 
3. Ensures eƯicient metabolism  and normal physiological functions like 
heartbeat, nerve conduction, and muscle contraction. 
4. Supports survival in varying environmental conditions by maintaining the 
oxygen-carbon dioxide balance . 
 
Signiﬁcance of Respiration 
Respiration is the process by which energy is released from food. Its importance 
includes: 
In Plants 
1. Releases energy from glucose  for: 
o Growth of roots, stems, leaves, ﬂowers, and fruits 
o Active transport of minerals 
o Cell division and elongation 
2. Provides energy for metabolic reactions , including synthesis of proteins, 
nucleic acids, and other biomolecules.

1. Provides energy (ATP)  for: 
o Muscle contraction and movement 
o Maintaining body temperature (thermoregulation) 
o Active transport of ions and nutrients in cells 
o Nerve impulse transmission 
2. Enables growth, repair, and reproduction  by supplying energy for anabolic 
reactions. 
3. Supports survival under low oxygen conditions (anaerobic respiration produces 
some energy when needed).

Plant Anatomy 
Deﬁnition:  
Plant anatomy is the study of the internal structure of plants , including tissues, 

--- PAGE 69 ---
 
Page | 69 cells, and organs. It focuses on how di Ưerent parts of a plant are organized to 
perform speciﬁc functions. 
Key Features:  
1. Tissues – Groups of cells with similar structure and function, e.g., xylem, 
phloem, parenchyma, collenchyma, sclerenchyma. 
2. Organs – Root, stem, leaves, ﬂowers, and fruits. 
3. Tissue Systems  – Three main systems: 
o Dermal system  – protective outer layer (epidermis, cuticle). 
o Ground system  – bulk of the plant (parenchyma, collenchyma,

o Vascular system  – transport of water, minerals, and food (xylem and

Importance of Anatomy:  
 Helps understand how plants absorb water, transport nutrients, and support 
themselves . 
 Useful in botany, agriculture, and plant breeding . 
 
Plant Physiology 
Deﬁnition:  
Plant physiology is the study of the functions and processes of plants , i.e., how 
plants live, grow, and respond to their environment. 
Key Processes:  
1. Photosynthesis  – Conversion of light energy into chemical energy (glucose). 
2. Respiration  – Release of energy from food for growth and metabolism. 
3. Transpiration  – Loss of water vapor from leaves; helps in nutrient transport and 
cooling. 
4. Translocation  – Movement of manufactured food from leaves to other parts of 
the plant. 
5. Gaseous Exchange  – Exchange of oxygen and carbon dioxide. 
6. Growth and Development  – Cell division, elongation, di Ưerentiation, and 
ﬂowering. 
Importance of Physiology:  
 Explains how plants obtain nutrients, produce food, and adapt  to their 
environment. 
 Helps improve crop yield, pest resistance, and stress tolerance . 
 
Summary 
 Anatomy  = structure (how plants are built). 
 Physiology  = function (how plants work). 
 Together, they provide a complete understanding of plant life .

 Functions:  
o Drink nectar e Ưiciently.

 Examples:  Hummingbird, Sunbird, Honeyeater.

3.2 Transport 
Transport in Animals 
Deﬁnition:  
Transport in animals is the movement of substances such as nutrients, gases, 
hormones, and waste products  from one part of the body to another. The 
circulatory system (blood or hemolymph) and the respiratory system play key 
roles. 
 
Importance of Transport 
1. Transport of Oxygen 
 Function:  Oxygen from the lungs or gills is carried to body cells for aerobic 
respiration . 
 Importance:  Provides energy (ATP) required for growth, movement, and 
metabolism. 
2. Transport of Carbon Dioxide 
 Function:  Carbon dioxide, a waste product of respiration, is carried from tissues 
to lungs/gills for removal. 
 Importance:  Prevents acidiﬁcation of body ﬂuids , maintaining blood pH. 
3. Transport of Nutrients 
 Function:  Digested food (glucose, amino acids, fatty acids) is transported from 
the gut to all body cells. 
 Importance:  Provides building blocks for growth, repair, and energy . 
4. Transport of Hormones 
 Function:  Hormones secreted by glands are carried in the blood to target 
organs. 


--- PAGE 77 ---
 
Page | 77  Importance:  Coordinates growth, reproduction, metabolism, and 
homeostasis . 
5. Transport of Metabolic Wastes 
 Function:  Waste products like urea and uric acid are transported to excretory 
organs (kidneys, liver). 
 Importance:  Prevents toxic accumulation  in tissues and maintains internal 
balance. 
6. Transport of Heat 
 Function:  Blood helps distribute heat from metabolically active organs to the 
whole body. 
 Importance:  Maintains body temperature  and prevents overheating or 
hypothermia. 
7. Transport of Immune Cells and Antibodies 
 Function:  White blood cells, antibodies, and other defense molecules are 
carried to sites of infection . 
 Importance:  Protects the body from diseases and pathogens . 
8. Transport for Communication and Coordination 
 Function:  Facilitates chemical signaling  (hormones) and nutrient delivery  for 
coordinated body function. 
 Importance:  Ensures eƯicient physiological regulation  across di Ưerent 
tissues.

Types of Circulatory Systems in Animals  
Animals have di Ưerent types of circulatory systems that transport nutrients, 
gases, and wastes throughout their bodies. These systems can be classiﬁed 
based on whether the blood is conﬁned to vessels and how many times it passes 
through the heart. 
1. Open Circulatory System  
 Structure:  Blood (or hemolymph) is not entirely contained in vessels. It ﬂows 
freely into body cavities and directly bathes organs. 
 Examples:  Insects, most mollusks, crustaceans. 
 Functions:  
o Transports nutrients and waste. 
o Helps maintain body pressure and support body structure (in some 
invertebrates). 
 Adaptations:  
o Hemolymph moves slowly; suitable for animals with low metabolic 
demands. 
o Simple heart or pumping organs to circulate ﬂuid. 
2. Closed Circulatory System  


--- PAGE 80 ---
 
Page | 80  Structure:  Blood is conﬁned to vessels (arteries, veins, capillaries) throughout 
the body. 
 Examples:  Earthworms, vertebrates (ﬁsh, amphibians, reptiles, birds, 
mammals). 
 Functions:  
o EƯicient transport of oxygen, nutrients, and wastes.  
o Supports high metabolic activity. 
 Adaptations:  
o Capillary networks increase surface area for exchange. 
o Blood pressure can be regulated. 
3. Single Circulatory System  
 Structure:  Blood passes through the heart once during each complete circuit of 
the body. 
 Examples:  Fish. 
 Features:  
o Heart has two chambers : one atrium, one ventricle. 
o Blood ﬂows from heart → gills → body → heart. 
o Blood pressure drops after gills, so circulation is slower. 
4. Double Circulatory System  
 Structure:  Blood passes through the heart twice during each complete circuit of 
the body. 
 Examples:  Amphibians, reptiles, birds, mammals. 
 Types: 
o Pulmonary circulation:  Heart → lungs → heart (oxygenation). 
o Systemic circulation:  Heart → body → heart (oxygen delivery). 
 Features:  
o Heart has three or four chambers  (amphibians/reptiles have three; 
birds/mammals have four). 
o Maintains higher blood pressure, supporting active metabolism.

Pumping Mechanism of the Mammalian Heart  
The mammalian heart is a four-chambered muscular organ  that pumps blood 
eƯiciently through a double circulatory system . Its pumping mechanism relies 
on coordinated contraction and relaxation of the atria and ventricles , regulated 
by electrical impulses. 
1. Structure of the Mammalian Heart Relevant to Pumping  
 Atria (Right and Left):  Upper chambers; receive blood from veins. 
o Right atrium: receives deoxygenated blood from the body (via superior 
and inferior vena cava). 
o Left atrium: receives oxygenated blood from the lungs (via pulmonary 
veins). 
 Ventricles (Right and Left):  Lower chambers; pump blood out of the heart. 


--- PAGE 82 ---
 
Page | 82 o Right ventricle: pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs (pulmonary 
artery). 
o Left ventricle: pumps oxygenated blood to the body (aorta). 
 Valves: Prevent backﬂow of blood. 
o Atrioventricular valves (tricuspid and mitral) between atria and ventricles. 
o Semilunar valves (pulmonary and aortic) at the exits of ventricles. 
2. Steps of the Pumping Mechanism (Cardiac Cycle)  
A. Atrial Systole (Contraction of Atria)  
 Atria contract, pushing blood into the relaxed ventricles. 
 AV valves open; semilunar valves closed. 
B. Ventricular Systole (Contraction of Ventricles)  
 Ventricles contract, increasing pressure and forcing blood out: 
o Right ventricle → pulmonary artery → lungs. 
o Left ventricle → aorta → body. 
 AV valves close to prevent backﬂow. 
 Semilunar valves open to allow blood ejection. 
C. Diastole (Relaxation Phase)  
 Both atria and ventricles relax. 
 Heart chambers reﬁll with blood: 
o Atria receive blood from veins. 
o Ventricles passively ﬁll from atria. 
 Semilunar valves close; AV valves open. 
3. Coordination of Pumping  
 Sinoatrial (SA) Node:  Natural pacemaker; generates electrical impulses 
initiating atrial contraction. 
 Atrioventricular (AV) Node:  Relays impulses to ventricles, causing coordinated 
ventricular contraction. 
 Purkinje Fibers:  Conduct impulses through ventricular walls for synchronized 
contraction. 
4. Summary of Blood Flow Through Heart  
1. Deoxygenated blood enters right atrium  → right ventricle → lungs (oxygenation). 
2. Oxygenated blood enters left atrium  → left ventricle → body tissues . 
3. Cycle repeats continuously to maintain circulation. 
5. Adaptations for E Ưicient Pumping  
 Thick muscular walls in ventricles (especially left) for strong contractions. 
 Valves prevent backﬂow, ensuring unidirectional ﬂow. 
 Electrical conduction system ensures rhythm and timing.

 Deﬁnition:  A network of vessels, tissues, and organs that help maintain ﬂuid

 Deﬁnition:  The body’s defense system against pathogens (bacteria, viruses,

 Components:  
o White Blood Cells (Leukocytes):

Blood Clotting Mechanisms (Hemostasis)  
 Deﬁnition:  The process that stops bleeding after blood vessel injury. 
 Phases of Blood Clotting:  
1. Vascular Spasm:  Blood vessels constrict to reduce blood loss. 
2. Platelet Plug Formation:  Platelets adhere to the damaged site and 
release chemicals that attract more platelets. 
3. Coagulation (Clotting Cascade):  
 Series of reactions activating clotting factors (proteins in plasma). 
 Prothrombin → Thrombin  (enzyme formation) 
 Fibrinogen → Fibrin  (insoluble protein threads that form the clot

repair begins. 
5. Fibrinolysis:  Clot is dissolved once the vessel is healed. 
 Important Components:  
o Platelets (Thrombocytes):  Initiate plug formation and release clotting 
factors. 
o Clotting Factors:  Proteins in plasma (I–XIII) activated in a cascade. 
o Calcium Ions and Vitamin K:  Essential cofactors for clotting reactions. 
 Signiﬁcance:  
o Prevents excessive blood loss. 
o Protects against infections entering through wounds.

Respiratory Surfaces in Animals  
Respiration in animals involves the exchange of gases (oxygen and carbon

Respiratory Surfaces in Animals  
Respiration in animals involves specialized surfaces adapted to e Ưiciently 
exchange gases (oxygen and carbon dioxide) with the environment. Di Ưerent 
animals have evolved di Ưerent respiratory structures based on their habitat, size, 
and metabolic needs. 
1. Insects  
 Respiratory System:  Tracheal system 
 Structure:  Network of tubes (tracheae) branching into ﬁner tubes (tracheoles) 
that reach individual cells. Open to the outside via spiracles . 
 Special Adaptations:  
o Respiratory siphons : Found in aquatic larvae like mosquito larvae; allow 
them to breathe air while submerged. 
o Tracheal gills : Present in some aquatic insects; thin, branched 
extensions of the tracheae that increase surface area for gas exchange in 
water. 
2. Fish 
 Respiratory System:  Gills 
 Structure:  Paired organs located on either side of the head; made up of gill 
arches, ﬁlaments, and lamellae. 
 Adaptations:  
o Large surface area through lamellae  for eƯicient diƯusion. 
o Counter-current ﬂow:  Water ﬂows opposite to blood in gill capillaries to 
maximize oxygen uptake. 
3. Amphibians  
 Respiratory Surfaces:  Lungs, buccal cavity, and skin (cutaneous respiration) 
 Adaptations:  
o Skin: Moist and vascularized for gas exchange; important when 
underwater or during hibernation. 
o Buccal cavity:  Air is drawn in and pushed into lungs using a “buccal 
pump.” 
o Lungs: Simple sac-like lungs with limited surface area; used mainly on 
land. 
4. Birds 
 Respiratory System:  Lungs with air sacs 
 Structure:  Small, rigid lungs connected to anterior and posterior air sacs; air 
ﬂows in one direction through parabronchi. 
 Adaptations:  
o EƯicient unidirectional airﬂow ensures continuous oxygen supply even 
during exhalation. 
o High metabolic rate adaptations for ﬂight. 
5. Mammals  
 Respiratory System:  Lungs

 Adaptations:  
o Alveoli: Tiny sacs with thin walls and rich capillary network for maximal 
gas exchange. 
o Diaphragm and intercostal muscles:  Facilitate ventilation. 
o Large surface area relative to volume for high oxygen demand. 
General Characteristics of Respiratory Surfaces in Animals  
Respiratory surfaces are specialized areas in animals where gas exchange 
(oxygen in, carbon dioxide out) occurs. Despite variations among animals, all 
eƯective respiratory surfaces share certain general characteristics:  
1. Large Surface Area  
 Increases the amount of gas that can di Ưuse at a time.  
 Examples: alveoli in mammals, lamellae in ﬁsh gills, tracheoles in insects. 
2. Thin and Permeable  
 Usually one cell thick or very thin to allow easy di Ưusion of gases.  
 Examples: alveolar walls, gill epithelium, insect tracheoles. 
3. Moist Surface  
 Gases dissolve in water before di Ưusing, so the surface must stay moist.  
 Important in amphibians’ skin, insect tracheoles, and ﬁsh gills. 
4. Well-Supplied with Blood (Vascularized) or Body Fluids  
 Maintains a concentration gradient for di Ưusion by transporting oxygen away and 
bringing carbon dioxide to the surface. 
 Examples: alveoli with capillaries, gill lamellae, amphibian skin. 
5. Adequate Ventilation  
 Fresh oxygen-rich medium (air or water) must continuously ﬂow over the surface. 
 Examples: lungs with breathing, gills with water ﬂow, tracheal system in insects. 
6. Thin and Delicate  
 Ensures minimal distance for di Ưusion of gases.  
 Surfaces are fragile but highly e Ưicient. 
7. Protection  
 Some respiratory surfaces have protective structures to prevent damage or 
drying while still allowing gas exchange. 
 Examples: tracheal spiracles can close, alveoli are inside lungs, gills covered by 
operculum in ﬁsh. 
These characteristics together ensure e Ưicient diƯusion of gases to meet the 
metabolic needs of the animal.

Mechanism of Gaseous Exchange in Animals  
Gaseous exchange is the movement of oxygen from the environment into the 
body and carbon dioxide from the body into the environment . The mechanism 
depends on the type of respiratory surface and the animal’s habitat. 
 

--- PAGE 92 ---
 
Page | 92 1. Insects (Spiracles and Tracheal System) 
 Mechanism:  
1. Air enters through spiracles . 
2. Passes through tracheae  and tracheoles  directly to cells. 
3. Oxygen diƯuses into cells  due to concentration gradient. 
4. Carbon dioxide di Ưuses from cells into tracheoles → tracheae → spiracles 
→ out. 
 Special Notes:  
o Active insects can use body movements  to pump air through tracheae. 
o Gas exchange occurs directly at the tissue level , not via blood. 
 
2. Fish (Gills) 
 Mechanism:  
1. Water enters mouth → passes over gill ﬁlaments and lamellae . 
2. Oxygen di Ưuses from water into blood capillaries  in gill lamellae. 
3. Carbon dioxide from blood di Ưuses into water.  
4. Counter-current ﬂow : water ﬂows opposite to blood, maintaining a 
steep concentration gradient for e Ưicient oxygen uptake.  
 
3. Amphibians (Lungs, Skin, Buccal Cavity) 
 Skin (Cutaneous Respiration):  
o Oxygen dissolves in moist skin , diƯuses into capillaries.  
o Carbon dioxide di Ưuses out the same way.  
 Lungs: 
o Air drawn into lungs by buccal pumping  (amphibians push air from 
mouth into lungs).

Process of Aerobic and Anaerobic Respiration  
Respiration is the process by which cells break down glucose to release 
energy (ATP) . There are two main types: aerobic (with oxygen) and anaerobic  
(without oxygen). 
 
1. Aerobic Respiration 
 Deﬁnition:  Breakdown of glucose in the presence of oxygen to release a large 
amount of energy. 
 General Equation (Word Form):  
Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water + Energy (ATP) 
 Chemical Equation (Symbol Form):  
C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + Energy (≈36–38 ATP molecules) 
 Process Steps:  
1. Glycolysis:  
 Occurs in the cytoplasm . 
 Glucose (6C) → 2 Pyruvate (3C) 
 Net gain: 2 ATP + 2 NADH 
2. Link Reaction (Pyruvate Decarboxylation):  
 Pyruvate → Acetyl-CoA + CO₂ 
 Occurs in mitochondrial matrix . 
3. Krebs Cycle (Citric Acid Cycle):  
 Acetyl-CoA oxidized → CO₂ 
 Produces NADH, FADH₂, and 2 ATP per glucose molecule. 
4. Electron Transport Chain (ETC) & Oxidative Phosphorylation:  
 Occurs in inner mitochondrial membrane . 
 NADH and FADH₂ donate electrons → energy used to pump H ⁺ → 
ATP synthesis. 
 Oxygen is the ﬁnal electron acceptor , forming water. 
 Characteristics/Adaptations:  
o Produces large energy yield  (36–38 ATP). 
o Requires continuous oxygen supply . 
o EƯicient in high-energy demand tissues  like muscles and brain. 
 
2. Anaerobic Respiration 
 Deﬁnition:  Breakdown of glucose without oxygen , releasing less energy. 

--- PAGE 94 ---
 
Page | 94  Equation (Word Form):  
Glucose → Lactic acid + Energy (in animals) 
Glucose → Ethanol + Carbon dioxide + Energy (in yeast/plants) 
 Chemical Equations:  
o In Animals:  C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2C₃H₆O₃ + 2 ATP 
o In Yeast/Plants:  C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2C₂H₅OH + 2CO₂ + 2 ATP 
 Process Steps:  
1. Glycolysis:  Glucose → 2 Pyruvate + 2 ATP (same as aerobic). 
2. Fermentation:  
 Animals:  Pyruvate → Lactic acid (builds up in muscles → fatigue). 
 Yeast/Plants:  Pyruvate → Ethanol + CO₂ (used in brewing/baking). 
 Characteristics/Adaptations:  
o Produces small energy yield  (2 ATP). 
o Does not require oxygen  → useful in low-oxygen conditions. 
o Produces by-products : lactic acid (muscles), ethanol & CO₂ (yeast).

Oxygen Debt  
Deﬁnition:  
Oxygen debt is the amount of extra oxygen that the body needs after vigorous 
exercise to restore the body to its normal resting state . It is also called excess 
post-exercise oxygen consumption (EPOC) . 
 
Causes of Oxygen Debt 
 During vigorous exercise , muscles may require more energy than aerobic 
respiration can supply . 
 As a result, muscles switch to anaerobic respiration  to produce ATP. 
 Anaerobic respiration produces lactic acid  instead of carbon dioxide and water. 
 After exercise, the body needs extra oxygen  to: 
1. Oxidize lactic acid  to carbon dioxide and water. 

--- PAGE 95 ---
 
Page | 95 2. Restore oxygen levels  in the blood and muscles (myoglobin stores). 
3. Replenish ATP and creatine phosphate  stores in muscles. 
 
EƯects of Oxygen Debt  
 Heavy breathing after exercise  (rapid and deep breaths) is the body’s way of 
repaying oxygen debt. 
 Gradually restores: 
o Normal oxygen concentration  in tissues. 
o Normal muscle function . 
o Energy stores  used during exercise. 
 
Summary of Oxygen Debt Process 
1. Vigorous exercise → increased energy demand → anaerobic respiration in 
muscles → lactic acid production.  
2. After exercise → extra oxygen needed → oxidation of lactic acid + restoration 
of energy reserves.  
3. Normal resting conditions restored.  
Example:  
 Sprinting, heavy weight lifting, or swimming quickly can cause oxygen debt. 
Factors A Ưecting Energy Requirement in Animals  
The energy requirement of an animal depends on several internal and external 
factors that inﬂuence metabolic rate  and the amount of energy needed for 
survival, growth, and activity. 
 
1. Body Size and Mass 
 Larger animals require more energy overall  because they have more cells to 
maintain. 
 Smaller animals may require more energy per gram of body weight  due to 
higher metabolic rates. 
2. Age 
 Young animals  require more energy for growth and development . 
 Adults require energy mainly for maintenance and activity. 
 Old animals  may require less energy due to slower metabolism. 
3. Activity Level 
 Active animals need more energy to power muscles . 
 Examples: Running, ﬂying, swimming, hunting. 
4. Environmental Temperature 
 Cold environments:  Animals expend more energy to maintain body 
temperature  (thermoregulation). 
 Hot environments:  Some energy is needed for cooling mechanisms , though 
usually less than in cold environments. 
5. Diet and Nutrition 
 Energy intake depends on the quantity and quality of food . 
 Animals consuming high-calorie diets  meet energy requirements more easily. 
6. Reproductive State 
 Pregnant or lactating females  require more energy to support fetal growth or 
milk production . 

--- PAGE 96 ---
 
Page | 96 7. Health Status 
 Diseases or infections  increase energy demand as the body ﬁghts pathogens 
and repairs tissues. 
8. Metabolic Rate 
 Animals with higher basal metabolic rates (BMR)  require more energy even at 
rest. 
 Factors a Ưecting BMR: species, genetics, hormones (e.g., thyroid hormones 
increase metabolism).

1. Respiratory Substrates 
Respiratory substrates are organic compounds oxidized in cells to release

a) Carbohydrates (e.g., glucose)  
 Preferred substrate  because they are easily oxidized. 
 Equation (aerobic respiration):

 Equation (generalized):  
C₅₅H₁₀₄O₆ + 78O₂ → 55CO₂ + 52H₂O + Energy 
c) Proteins (e.g., amino acids)  
 Used only when carbohydrates and fats are insu Ưicient. 
 Amino acids are deaminated  before entering respiration. 
Adaptations:  
 Cells preferentially use carbohydrates  for rapid energy. 
 Fats are used for long-term energy storage . 
 Proteins are last resort substrates .
