Cell Structure & Function

Introduction to Cells

The cell is the basic unit of life. All living organisms are composed of one or more cells. Cells arise from pre-existing cells through cell division. There are two main types of cells: prokaryotic and eukaryotic.

Types of Cells

Prokaryotic cells lack a membrane-bound nucleus and organelles. They are typically smaller and simpler than eukaryotic cells. Examples include bacteria and archaea.

Eukaryotic cells contain a membrane-bound nucleus and various organelles. These cells are larger and more complex. They are found in animals, plants, and fungi.

Cell Components

  • Cell Membrane: A semi-permeable barrier that controls the movement of substances in and out of the cell
  • Nucleus: Contains the cell's DNA and controls cell activities
  • Mitochondria: The powerhouse of the cell, responsible for energy production
  • Ribosomes: Sites of protein synthesis
  • Endoplasmic Reticulum: Network of membranes involved in protein and lipid synthesis
  • Golgi Apparatus: Modifies and packages proteins for transport

Cell Division

Cells grow and divide through two main processes: mitosis and meiosis. Mitosis produces two identical daughter cells for growth and repair, while meiosis produces four genetically different cells for sexual reproduction.

Chapter 2

Photosynthesis & Energy

Photosynthesis Overview

Photosynthesis is the process by which plants convert light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose. This process occurs primarily in the leaves of plants.

Light-Dependent Reactions

These reactions occur in the thylakoid membranes of the chloroplast. Light energy is absorbed by chlorophyll and used to split water molecules, releasing oxygen and electrons. The electrons are used to generate ATP and NADPH.

Light-Independent Reactions (Calvin Cycle)

The Calvin Cycle occurs in the stroma of the chloroplast. Carbon dioxide is fixed into 3-phosphoglycerate, which is then reduced using ATP and NADPH. This produces glucose, which is used by the plant for energy and growth.

Factors Affecting Photosynthesis

  • Light Intensity: More light increases photosynthesis rate up to a saturation point
  • Temperature: Enzymes work optimally at around 25-35°C
  • CO₂ Concentration: Higher CO₂ increases photosynthesis rate
  • Water Availability: Essential for the light-dependent reactions

Chapter 3

Genetics & Heredity

DNA Structure

DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) is a double helix molecule composed of nucleotides. Each nucleotide contains a phosphate group, a sugar molecule (deoxyribose), and a nitrogenous base.

Inheritance

Genes are segments of DNA that code for proteins and traits. They are passed from parents to offspring through sexual reproduction. Alleles are different versions of the same gene.

Mendelian Genetics

Gregor Mendel discovered the principles of inheritance through his experiments with pea plants. The law of segregation states that alleles separate during gamete formation. The law of independent assortment states that different genes are inherited independently.

Dominant and Recessive Traits

Dominant alleles hide the effect of recessive alleles. An organism with two dominant alleles (DD) or one dominant and one recessive (Dd) shows the dominant trait. Only organisms with two recessive alleles (dd) show the recessive trait.

Mitosis vs Meiosis

Mitosis produces two identical diploid cells for growth and repair, while meiosis produces four genetically different haploid cells for sexual reproduction.
